Before you read
Whose Australia are we talking about?
Australia is one of the most multicultural countries on earth. More than half of all Australians were either born overseas, or have at least one parent who was. Melbourne has large Vietnamese, Chinese, Lebanese, Greek, and Indian communities who have shaped the city profoundly. First- and second-generation Australians often navigate two cultural codes at once.

And beneath all of this: Indigenous Australians have lived on this continent for over 65,000 years — the world's oldest continuing cultures. Most Australians who pride themselves on mateship and a fair go have never had a conversation with an Indigenous Australian. That gap between the national self-image and the reality is one of the most significant unresolved facts about the country.
When we describe "Australian norms" — whose norms are we actually describing? And who gets to decide what belonging looks like?
Scene 1 · Melbourne, first day of work experience
The compliment
Leo (from Germany) starts work experience at a Melbourne marketing company. He's been in Australia three years and his English is fluent. His supervisor Jade introduces herself.
JadeHey Leo! Welcome — really glad to have you here.
LeoThanks, Jade. I've actually looked into the company quite a bit — the results your team got last year were seriously impressive. You must be really proud of what you've built.
Jadesmall laugh, glances at her shoes Ha — yeah, cheers. We just got lucky, honestly. Anyway — let me show you your desk.
What went wrong?
Leo's English is fine. His intentions are good. But a sincere, specific compliment directed at someone you've just met makes Australians uncomfortable. It puts them on the spot — it feels like a performance review rather than a greeting.

Jade's response — deflecting, attributing success to luck, physically looking away — is completely standard. It's not false modesty. It's the tall poppy reflex: no one should stand too far above the crowd, including when someone is trying to lift them there. Leo meant to connect. He created distance instead.
Your turn: Rewrite Leo's line. Same warmth, different approach — what does he say, and what does he leave out entirely?
Scene 2 · Melbourne, pub after work
The round
Priya moved to Australia from India three years ago and started at a new company last month. Four colleagues invite her for drinks on Friday. It's her first time out with them.
TomRight — I'll grab the first round. What are we having?
PriyaOh — honestly, don't worry about me, I can just— sorry, I don't want to— I'll just get my own, it's easier—
TomNah, come on. What are you having?
Priyaquietly Just a lemon squash, thanks.
Tomto the others, once she turns away Is she all right?
What went wrong?
Priya isn't being rude — she's flustered. She doesn't have the script for this situation and it shows. But the effect is the same: she's declined to enter a social system she didn't know existed.

Shouting rounds isn't personal generosity. Tom isn't being kind to Priya specifically — he's opening a rotation: I buy this round, you buy the next. Everyone participates. Refusing to enter the system — even awkwardly, even politely — reads as not wanting to be part of the group.

The right move is simple: accept the drink, and signal you understand the contract.
Your turn: Replace Priya's first response with one line that works. Here's a model: "Can't this week, but I'm in next time." — what does that line do that Priya's version doesn't?
Scene 3 · Melbourne, Friday afternoon, office
The sweep
It's Melbourne Cup day. Marek is in his third week at an Australian company. He drinks, happily. He has seen things. But this is new.
JessMarek — you in the sweep? Five bucks, random horse.
MarekSorry — the sweep?
JessMelbourne Cup. Horse race. You get a random horse, five bucks.
Bossfrom across the room, already holding a beer at 3pm Marek, you have to do the sweep. It's basically illegal not to.
Mareklooking at the colleagues with drinks, the TAB app open on Jess's phone, the boss ...Is this normal?
JessIt's the Cup, mate.
What's actually happening here?
Marek isn't shocked by the drinking — he's Czech. He's shocked by everything else.

Australia has the highest gambling losses per capita in the world. But the gambling isn't in casinos — it's in pubs, sports clubs, and on your phone via the TAB, a government-run betting agency as unremarkable as a post office. The Melbourne Cup sweep is so embedded in workplace culture that opting out feels genuinely antisocial.

The boss having a beer at 3pm on a Friday is also completely normal. The line between pub culture and workplace culture in Australia is much thinner than in most of Europe. None of this registers as unusual. Whether it should be is a different question.
Your turn: Marek decides to join in. Write his next two lines — how does he enter the sweep in a way that sounds natural rather than reluctant or overly formal?

What the Czech student got wrong

Australian friendliness isn't performance. It comes from a genuine belief that everyone deserves a fair go — regardless of background or status. When a stranger chats to you in a queue, or a barista asks how you're going, that warmth is real.

But it goes sideways, not toward you. It says we're all in this together — not I want you to like me. American warmth is expressive and aimed at you: effusive, directed, performing intimacy it may not mean. Australian warmth doesn't do that. The Czech student was reading the absence of escalation as the absence of feeling. That's the misread.

What the Czech student got right

Australian friendliness is wide and doesn't go deep quickly.

Turning easy warmth into actual friendship takes time, consistency, and repeated low-pressure contact — not one intense bonding moment. The warmth is genuine. It is also genuinely not an invitation to get close. Both things are true at once, and that's exactly what the Czech student found confusing. They weren't wrong. They were just expecting a different architecture.

The bigger picture

Dr Anne Aly — an Egyptian-Australian Muslim woman, Australia's Minister for Multicultural Affairs, and the first Muslim woman elected to Australia's federal parliament — has said: "To those few who say immigrants should 'go home,' I say: we are home."

More than half of all Australians were born overseas or have a parent who was. And yet most Australians who pride themselves on mateship and the fair go have never had a conversation with an Indigenous Australian — whose cultures have been here for over 65,000 years. The national self-image and the national reality are still some distance apart.
